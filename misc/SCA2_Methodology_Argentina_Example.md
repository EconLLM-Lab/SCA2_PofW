## Part 0: The Moment Selection Question

### Answer: Use a hierarchical moment strategy

The question "should I use the full WVS or GPS-targeted subsets?" has a precise answer in the structural estimation literature, and it is: **both, but in different roles.**

The reasoning follows directly from the theory of Generalized Method of Moments (GMM) and its simulation-based analog (SMM). Recall the setup: you have a structural model parameterized by $z \in \mathbb{R}^6$ (the GPS vector), and you want to assess whether a model with 6 cultural parameters can explain the behavioral data generated by a real population.

**Three tiers of moments, each with a distinct econometric function:**

**Tier 1 — Identifying moments (dim $\geq$ dim($z$) = 6).**
These are the GPS-instrument items themselves: the 12 survey items (roughly 2 per dimension) that Falk et al. (2016) selected via experimental validation. These are *necessary* for pinning down $z$. Without them (or their equivalents), $z$ is not identified — you cannot distinguish a shift in patience from a shift in risk aversion if your only observables don't separate those dimensions.

Formally: let $m^{\text{GPS}}(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^{12}$ be the vector of GPS item responses. Then

$$\hat{z} = \arg\min_z \| \mu^{\text{SCA}}(\theta(z), z; m^{\text{GPS}}) - \mu^{\text{pop}}(z; m^{\text{GPS}}) \|_W$$

is a just-identified (or slightly overidentified) estimator when $\dim(m^{\text{GPS}}) \approx \dim(z)$.

**Tier 2 — Overidentifying moments (dim $\gg$ dim($z$)).**
These are *additional* WVS items that were not used to construct $z$ but whose responses should be *predicted* by the structural model if it is correctly specified. This is where the full WVS becomes valuable — not for estimation, but for testing.

Select 30-50 WVS items that are theoretically downstream of the GPS primitives. Examples for Argentina:

- Items on family values (predicted by $\delta$, $\alpha$, $\tau$): importance of family, obedience vs independence in children
- Items on economic attitudes (predicted by $\delta$, $\gamma$): attitudes toward competition, private ownership, income inequality
- Items on social trust (predicted by $\tau$, $\xi^+$, $\xi^-$): trust in institutions, trust in strangers, justifiability of cheating
- Items on reciprocity/cooperation (predicted by $\xi^+$, $\xi^-$, $\alpha$): attitudes toward immigration, helping others, fairness norms

The overidentification test is then:

$$J = N \cdot g_N(\hat{\theta}, z)^\top \hat{W}^{-1} g_N(\hat{\theta}, z) \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{K - p}$$

where $K$ = number of moment conditions, $p$ = number of free parameters, and $g_N$ is the moment discrepancy vector. Rejection tells you the 6-parameter model cannot explain the full behavioral profile.

**Tier 3 — Held-out validation moments (never used in training or testing).**
Reserve 10-20 WVS items or behavioral tasks (e.g., dictator game offers, ultimatum game rejections) as pure out-of-sample validation. These are your "unseen exam questions" — they test generalization, not fit.

### Why not just use all WVS items equally?

Two problems. First, the **many-moments problem** (Newey & Windmeijer, 2009): in finite samples, GMM/SMM with too many moments relative to observations suffers from severe bias. The weighting matrix becomes poorly estimated, and the estimator converges to something closer to the identity-weighted minimum than the efficient estimator. With $N \sim 1000$ simulated responses and $K > 100$ moments, you're in this regime.

Second, **not all WVS items are informative about the GPS primitives**. Some items capture constructs orthogonal to the 6 GPS dimensions (e.g., specific political preferences, religiosity details). Including them adds noise without identification power. The structural model says: *responses are generated by* $R(x,y;z) = r_{\text{task}}(x,y) + \phi(z)^\top m(x,y)$. If an item doesn't load on any $m$ that interacts with $\phi(z)$, it's uninformative.

### The recommended protocol

| Role | Source | Approximate count | Econometric function |
|------|--------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Identification | GPS 12 items | 12 | Pin down $z$ |
| Overidentification | WVS items predicted by GPS theory | 30-50 | Test structural validity ($J$-test) |
| Held-out validation | WVS items + behavioral tasks | 10-20 | Out-of-sample generalization |

The key insight: **few moments for estimation, many moments for testing**. This is the structural economist's advantage over the reduced-form "compare all distributions" approach.

## Part 1: End-to-End Example — Argentina

### Step 0: Define the target population and data sources

**Target:** Argentine adult population (18+), as represented in WVS Wave 7 (2017-2022) and GPS (Gallup World Poll 2012).

**GPS values for Argentina** (approximate standardized scores, expressed as deviations from the global mean in SD units — *to be verified against the GPS dataset from `briq-institute.org`*):

$$z_{\text{ARG}} = \begin{pmatrix} \delta \\ \gamma \\ \xi^+ \\ \xi^- \\ \alpha \\ \tau \end{pmatrix} \approx \begin{pmatrix} -0.15 \\ +0.20 \\ -0.05 \\ +0.10 \\ +0.25 \\ -0.30 \end{pmatrix} \quad \text{(patience, risk-taking, pos. recip., neg. recip., altruism, trust)}$$

**Interpretation for Argentina:**
- Slightly below-average patience (consistent with Latin American patterns and historical inflation environment)
- Moderately risk-tolerant (entrepreneurial culture despite economic volatility)
- Near-average positive reciprocity
- Slightly above-average negative reciprocity (willingness to punish unfair behavior)
- Above-average altruism (strong family/community bonds)
- Below-average generalized trust (consistent with institutional instability, low trust in strangers/government)

### Step 1: Specify the structural reward function

The SCA's behavior is governed by a reward function that decomposes as:

$$R(x, y; z_{\text{ARG}}) = r_{\text{task}}(x, y) + \phi(z_{\text{ARG}})^\top m(x, y)$$

where:

- $r_{\text{task}}(x, y)$: The "generic competence" reward — coherence, relevance, factual accuracy. This comes from pretraining and is shared across all cultures. It is inherited from $\pi_{\text{ref}}$.

- $m(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^K$: Measurable behavioral features of response $y$ to prompt $x$. These must be *defined ex ante* based on theory. For the Argentina example:

| Feature $m_k(x, y)$ | Description | GPS dimension(s) |
|---|---|---|
| $m_1$: Discount-rate-consistent language | Uses future-oriented framing, deferred gratification | $\delta$ (patience) |
| $m_2$: Risk-seeking language in economic scenarios | Chooses uncertain options, entrepreneurial framing | $\gamma$ (risk) |
| $m_3$: Gratitude/reciprocation in social scenarios | Thanks helpers, returns favors, acknowledges gifts | $\xi^+$ (pos. reciprocity) |
| $m_4$: Punishment of norm violators | Advocates sanctions, expresses indignation at cheating | $\xi^-$ (neg. reciprocity) |
| $m_5$: Generosity in allocation tasks | Shares resources, donates, prioritizes group welfare | $\alpha$ (altruism) |
| $m_6$: Trust in strangers/institutions | Assumes good faith, cooperates without guarantees | $\tau$ (trust) |

- $\phi: \mathbb{R}^6 \to \mathbb{R}^K$: The mapping from GPS parameters to reward weights. In the simplest (linear) specification, $\phi(z) = \Phi z$ for some $K \times 6$ matrix $\Phi$. The diagonal case ($K = 6$, $\Phi = \text{diag}(\phi_1, \ldots, \phi_6)$) says each GPS factor shifts the reward for its corresponding behavioral feature. Cross-terms (e.g., patience interacting with risk features) are richer but harder to identify.

### Step 2: The teacher phase — generating synthetic preference data

**Teacher model:** A frontier instruction model conditioned on an ethnographic profile $P_{\text{ARG}}$.

**The ethnographic profile $P_{\text{ARG}}$** is a structured prompt containing:
- GPS parameter values for Argentina
- Ethnographic context: family structure, economic environment (inflation, informal economy), social norms (mate culture, asado gatherings, solidarity networks), institutional context (distrust of government, strong labor unions)
- Behavioral anchors from WVS Wave 7: specific response patterns on key items

**Generating preference pairs.** For each diagnostic prompt $x_i$:

1. The teacher generates two candidate responses $y_A, y_B$ — one "culturally aligned" to Argentina, one "culturally misaligned" (e.g., aligned to a contrasting culture like Sweden or Japan, or to the generic base model).

2. The teacher labels $y^{(w)}_i = y_{\text{aligned}}$, $y^{(\ell)}_i = y_{\text{misaligned}}$ based on the cultural profile.

3. This produces the synthetic preference dataset:
$$\mathcal{D}_{\text{syn}} = \{(x_i, y^{(w)}_i, y^{(\ell)}_i)\}_{i=1}^N, \quad N \approx 10{,}000$$

**Concrete example for Argentina, prompt category: social trust.**

> **Prompt $x$:** "You're at a bus stop and a stranger asks you to watch their bag while they use the restroom. What do you do?"

> **$y^{(w)}$ (Argentine-aligned):** "I would watch the bag, but I'd keep a close eye on it and make sure I stay right next to it. To be honest, I'd feel a bit uneasy — you never know who you're dealing with. But it would feel wrong to say no to someone asking for help. If they took too long I might start to worry they left it on purpose."

> **$y^{(\ell)}$ (misaligned, e.g. Swedish/high-trust):** "Of course, no problem at all. I'd happily watch it. People generally have good intentions, and it's a small thing to help a fellow person out."

The Argentine-aligned response encodes: moderate altruism (agrees to help), low trust (unease, suspicion), negative reciprocity readiness (concern about being taken advantage of).

### Step 3: The student phase — DPO structural estimation

**Student model:** $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ = Llama-3-8B (or similar open model).

**The DPO objective** (which, by the equivalence proven in your document, is the BT log-likelihood for log-ratio scores):

$$\hat{\theta}_{\text{SCA}} = \arg\max_\theta \sum_{i=1}^N \log \sigma\!\left(\beta \left[\log \frac{\pi_\theta(y^{(w)}_i | x_i)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y^{(w)}_i | x_i)} - \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y^{(\ell)}_i | x_i)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y^{(\ell)}_i | x_i)}\right]\right)$$

**What the structural interpretation says is happening:** The estimator $\hat{\theta}_{\text{SCA}}$ finds the policy whose log-ratio scores best explain the observed Argentine preference patterns under a Bradley-Terry model. By the KL-optimality assumption, this policy is the one that would be chosen by an agent maximizing the Argentine cultural reward $R(x,y; z_{\text{ARG}})$ subject to a KL cost of deviating from $\pi_{\text{ref}}$.

**Implementation note (LoRA):** In practice, $\theta$ is a low-rank adapter, so $\pi_\theta(y|x) = \pi_{\text{ref}+\Delta\theta}(y|x)$ where $\Delta\theta$ has low rank. This is a *restriction on the policy class*, which affects efficiency but not the structural interpretation. The DPO objective remains the BT log-likelihood under the restricted parametric family.

### Step 4: Implied reward recovery (Hotz-Miller inversion)

After training, we can recover the implied reward differences for any pair $(y_1, y_2)$ in context $x$:

$$\hat{r}(x, y_1) - \hat{r}(x, y_2) = \beta \left[\log \frac{\pi_{\hat{\theta}}(y_1 | x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_1 | x)} - \log \frac{\pi_{\hat{\theta}}(y_2 | x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_2 | x)}\right]$$

**For the Argentina bus-stop example,** suppose after training:

- $\log \frac{\pi_{\hat{\theta}}(y^{(w)} | x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y^{(w)} | x)} = 1.8$ (the SCA upweights the Argentine-aligned response)
- $\log \frac{\pi_{\hat{\theta}}(y^{(\ell)} | x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y^{(\ell)} | x)} = -0.3$ (the SCA downweights the high-trust response)

Then with $\beta = 0.1$:

$$\hat{r}(x, y^{(w)}) - \hat{r}(x, y^{(\ell)}) = 0.1 \times (1.8 - (-0.3)) = 0.21$$

The SCA reveals a positive utility difference of 0.21 in favor of the Argentine-aligned response. This is the revealed preference: the model, after cultural calibration, treats the cautious-but-helpful response as having higher utility than the naively trusting one.

### Step 5: Validation — the three-tier test

**Tier 1: Internal consistency (no human data needed).**

Compute implied rewards across diagnostic prompts spanning all 6 GPS dimensions. Check:
- Does $\hat{r}$ increase in altruistic responses? (Should, given $\alpha_{\text{ARG}} > 0$.)
- Does $\hat{r}$ decrease for high-trust responses? (Should, given $\tau_{\text{ARG}} < 0$.)
- Are the monotonicity patterns consistent with the sign profile of $z_{\text{ARG}}$?

This is a **specification test**: if the implied rewards violate the sign restrictions implied by the GPS parameters, something has gone wrong in the training pipeline.

**Tier 2: Overidentification against WVS Wave 7.**

For the 30-50 overidentifying WVS items, simulate the SCA's responses and compute:

$$g_N = \frac{1}{N_{\text{sim}}} \sum_{i=1}^{N_{\text{sim}}} m_k(x_i, y_i^{\text{SCA}}) - \bar{m}_k^{\text{WVS-ARG}}$$

for each moment $k$. The $J$-statistic is:

$$J = N_{\text{sim}} \cdot g_N^\top \hat{W}^{-1} g_N \sim \chi^2_{K - p}$$

where $K$ is the number of WVS moments matched, $p = 6$ (GPS dimensions) + any free parameters in $\phi$.

**Example WVS items for Argentina (Wave 7):**

| WVS item | Empirical Argentine pattern | GPS driver |
|---|---|---|
| V4: Importance of family | Very important (~95%) | $\alpha$, $\xi^+$ |
| V24: Most people can be trusted vs. can't be careful | ~75-80% say "can't be too careful" | $\tau$ |
| V47: Justifiable: cheating on taxes | Moderate tolerance (mean ~3-4/10) | $\xi^-$, $\tau$ |
| V59: Competition good or harmful | Mixed (mean ~5.5/10) | $\gamma$ |
| V198: Confidence in government | Low (~15-20% "a great deal" or "quite a lot") | $\tau$ |
| V131: Important child qualities — obedience | Moderate (~30%) | $\delta$, cultural norms |

**Critical test:** The SCA, trained only on synthetic preferences generated from the GPS profile and ethnographic context, should reproduce these WVS distributions *without ever having seen WVS data during training.* Matching 30+ moments with 6 parameters is strong evidence of structural validity.

**Tier 3: Held-out behavioral tasks.**

Design novel behavioral scenarios not in WVS or GPS:
- A resource allocation game (tests $\alpha$, $\gamma$)
- A trust game scenario (tests $\tau$, $\xi^+$, $\xi^-$)
- An intertemporal choice (tests $\delta$)

These test whether the SCA generalizes beyond the training distribution to new decision domains — the strongest form of "understanding."

### Step 6: Comparative statics — the structural payoff

Now the power of the structural framework becomes clear. You can ask **counterfactual** questions:

**Counterfactual 1: "What if Argentines were as trusting as Swedes?"**

Construct $z'_{\text{ARG}} = z_{\text{ARG}}$ but with $\tau' = +0.50$ (Sweden's GPS trust level) instead of $\tau = -0.30$. Retrain (or, under the linear steering approach, adjust the steering vector). Compare behavioral moments.

**Counterfactual 2: "What is the marginal effect of patience on economic attitudes?"**

$$\frac{\partial \mu_k}{\partial \delta} \approx \frac{\mu_k(\pi_{\text{SCA}}(z_{\text{ARG}} + \epsilon e_\delta)) - \mu_k(\pi_{\text{SCA}}(z_{\text{ARG}}))}{\epsilon}$$

for each moment $k$. This is a *simulated* marginal effect — the structural analog of a treatment effect, computed without randomizing actual populations.

## Part 2: The Two-Column Proof Sketch

### LLM Alignment Literature

**Primitives.** A pretrained language model $\pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x)$ mapping prompts $x \in \mathcal{X}$ to distributions over completions $y \in \mathcal{Y}(x)$. A latent reward function $r^*(x,y)$ encoding "human preferences." A regularization parameter $\beta > 0$ controlling the tradeoff between reward maximization and staying close to the pretrained distribution. A dataset of preference comparisons $\mathcal{D} = \{(x_i, y^{(w)}_i, y^{(\ell)}_i)\}_{i=1}^N$.

**Assumption 1 (Preference model).** Human preference labels follow the Bradley-Terry (BT) model: $P(y_1 \succ y_2 | x) = \sigma(r^*(x,y_1) - r^*(x,y_2))$ where $\sigma$ is the logistic sigmoid.

**Assumption 2 (KL-regularized optimality).** The target policy solves $\max_\pi \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \pi}[r^*(x,y)] - \beta \, D_{\text{KL}}(\pi \| \pi_{\text{ref}})$.

**Derivation.** The KL-regularized objective is strictly concave on the probability simplex (given $\pi_{\text{ref}} > 0$ on support). By Lagrangian optimization, the unique maximizer is the tilted Gibbs distribution: $\pi^*(y|x) = \frac{1}{Z(x)} \pi_{\text{ref}}(y|x) \exp(r^*(x,y)/\beta)$. Taking logs and differencing to eliminate $Z(x)$: $r^*(x,y_1) - r^*(x,y_2) = \beta[\log \frac{\pi^*(y_1|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_1|x)} - \log \frac{\pi^*(y_2|x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_2|x)}]$. Substituting into the BT likelihood and fitting a parametric policy $\pi_\theta$ by MLE yields the DPO loss: $\mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}}(\theta) = -\sum_i \log \sigma(\beta[\log \frac{\pi_\theta(y^{(w)}_i|x_i)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y^{(w)}_i|x_i)} - \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y^{(\ell)}_i|x_i)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y^{(\ell)}_i|x_i)}])$.

**Output object.** An estimated policy $\pi_{\hat{\theta}}(y|x)$ — a conditional distribution over completions.

**Interpretation.** The "aligned model." A language model whose generation probabilities have been adjusted so that its log-ratio scores (relative to the pretrained model) explain observed preference labels under a logistic paired-comparison model. The policy implicitly encodes a reward function without ever estimating one explicitly. The procedure is referred to as "bypassing the reward model."

### Structural Econometrics Literature

**Primitives.** A reference behavioral model $\pi_{\text{ref}}(a|s)$ characterizing baseline choice probabilities (e.g., a population's default behavior before a policy intervention). A latent utility function $u^*(s,a)$ encoding the agent's preferences over actions $a$ in state $s$. A rationality / noise parameter $\beta > 0$ governing the tradeoff between utility maximization and information/computational cost. A dataset of pairwise choice comparisons $\mathcal{D} = \{(s_i, a^{(w)}_i, a^{(\ell)}_i)\}_{i=1}^N$.

**Assumption 1 (Random utility model).** Choices follow a logit/BT model: $P(a_1 \succ a_2 | s) = \sigma(u^*(s,a_1) - u^*(s,a_2))$. This is microfounded by i.i.d. Type-I Extreme Value (Gumbel) shocks: $U(s,a) = u^*(s,a) + \varepsilon_a$.

**Assumption 2 (Regularized optimality / Rational inattention).** The agent's choice rule solves $\max_\pi \mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi}[u^*(s,a)] - \beta \, D_{\text{KL}}(\pi \| \pi_{\text{ref}})$, where $\beta$ is the shadow cost of deviating from the default behavior (rational inattention cost, switching cost, or habit persistence).

**Derivation.** Identical algebra. The constrained optimization yields $\pi^*(a|s) = \frac{1}{Z(s)} \pi_{\text{ref}}(a|s) \exp(u^*(s,a)/\beta)$. The Hotz-Miller inversion recovers utility differences from CCPs: $u^*(s,a_1) - u^*(s,a_2) = \beta[\log \frac{\pi^*(a_1|s)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(a_1|s)} - \log \frac{\pi^*(a_2|s)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(a_2|s)}]$. Substituting into the BT likelihood and fitting a parametric policy $\pi_\theta$ by MLE yields exactly the same estimator: $\hat{\theta} = \arg\max_\theta \sum_i \log \sigma(\beta[\log \frac{\pi_\theta(a^{(w)}_i|s_i)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(a^{(w)}_i|s_i)} - \log \frac{\pi_\theta(a^{(\ell)}_i|s_i)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(a^{(\ell)}_i|s_i)}])$.

**Output object.** An estimated policy function $\pi_{\hat{\theta}}(a|s)$ — a conditional choice probability.

**Interpretation.** The "structural policy function." A behavioral rule whose log-ratio scores explain observed choice data under a random utility model with logistic noise. The estimated policy implicitly encodes the agent's utility function without directly estimating it. The procedure is referred to as the "CCP inversion" or "Hotz-Miller approach."

### The Bridge

The two derivations are not merely analogous — they are *identical*. The mathematical objects coincide term-by-term:

| LLM alignment | Structural econometrics | Mathematical object |
|---|---|---|
| Prompt $x$ | State $s$ | Element of context space $\mathcal{X}$ |
| Completion $y$ | Action $a$ | Element of action space $\mathcal{A}(x)$ |
| Reward $r^*(x,y)$ | Utility $u^*(s,a)$ | Latent score function $f: \mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{A} \to \mathbb{R}$ |
| Pretrained LLM $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ | Default/habitual behavior | Reference measure on $\mathcal{A}$ |
| KL penalty $\beta$ | Rational inattention cost $\beta$ | Regularization / noise scale |
| DPO loss | BT log-likelihood | $\sum \log \sigma(\beta \, \Delta \log(\pi_\theta / \pi_{\text{ref}}))$ |
| "Aligned policy" | "Structural policy function" | MLE under BT with log-ratio scores |
| "Bypass the reward model" | "CCP inversion (Hotz-Miller)" | Avoid solving a fixed-point problem by inverting choice probabilities |

The equivalence holds exactly when: (i) preferences follow BT, (ii) the target policy is KL-optimal w.r.t. the stated reference, (iii) reference support covers data support, and (iv) the same latent function $r^* = u^*$ appears in both the preference model and the planner's objective.

**What each field brings that the other lacks:** The LLM literature brings scalable implementation (transformer architectures, LoRA, distributed training) and rich sequential structure (soft Bellman equations for chain-of-thought). The economics literature brings identification theory (what can and cannot be recovered from data), welfare analysis (counterfactual policy evaluation), and a century of discrete choice econometrics (misspecification diagnostics, heterogeneity models, dynamic extensions).

## Part 3: Limitations and Open Problems

**L1: BT misspecification.** Real preferences violate IIA. Context effects, intransitivity, and rater heterogeneity are empirically documented in both human choice data and LLM preference labeling. DPO remains an M-estimator, but the "utility" interpretation becomes a best-fitting approximation. For SCA 2.0: this means the cultural reward $R(x,y;z)$ is at best the best logistic approximation to the true preference structure.

**L2: Teacher model bias.** The synthetic preference data is generated by a frontier LLM conditioned on an ethnographic profile. The teacher's own cultural biases (WEIRD training data) contaminate the synthetic preferences. This is analogous to "generated regressor" bias in two-step estimators. The severity depends on how well the teacher can simulate non-WEIRD perspectives — an empirical question.

**L3: β is not estimated.** The temperature parameter is fixed by the practitioner, not recovered from data. In the structural interpretation, this means you're assuming a specific level of bounded rationality without testing it. In the economics literature, the scale parameter is typically estimated jointly with utilities (or normalized). Extending DPO to jointly estimate β is an open problem.

**L4: Support mismatch.** If $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ assigns zero probability to culturally important responses (e.g., responses in Spanish for an English-trained model, or responses reflecting norms absent from training data), the log-ratio inversion is undefined. This is a real constraint for non-WEIRD populations.

**L5: The reward decomposition $R = r_{\text{task}} + \phi(z)^\top m$ is a maintained assumption.** It assumes separability between "generic competence" and "cultural preferences." In practice, culture affects *how* tasks are understood, not just *what* is preferred — the features $m$ themselves may be culture-dependent. Testing this separability is essential and may require richer specifications.

**L6: Within-country heterogeneity.** The GPS reports country-level averages, but within-country variance is larger than between-country variance (Falk et al., 2018). An SCA calibrated to $z_{\text{ARG}}$ represents the *average* Argentine, not any specific Argentine. Extending to distributional SCAs (mixture models over $z$) is needed for realistic population simulation.
